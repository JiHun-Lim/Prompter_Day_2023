Problem 1 (Chapter: 1.5)

Find the exact value of \(\cos(2\sin^{-1}(12/13))\).

Answer:

\(-119/169 \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} \text{Step 1:} \quad & \text{We know that } \cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2(\theta). \\ & \text{We can use this identity to simplify the problem.} \\ \text{Step 2:} \quad & \text{Let } \theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{12}{13}\right). \\ & \text{This means that } \sin(\theta) = \frac{12}{13}. \\ \text{Step 3:} \quad & \text{We can use the Pythagorean identity,} \\ & \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1, \text{ to find } \cos(\theta). \\ & \text{Solving for } \cos(\theta), \text{ we get} \\ & \cos(\theta) = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2(\theta)} \\ & = \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{12}{13}\right)^2} \\ & = \sqrt{1 - \frac{144}{169}} \\ & = \sqrt{\frac{25}{169}} \\ & = \frac{5}{13}. \\ \text{Step 4:} \quad & \text{Now we can substitute } \theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{12}{13}\right) \\ & \text{into the identity } \cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2(\theta) \text{ to get} \\ & \cos\left(2\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{12}{13}\right)\right) \\ & = 1 - 2\left(\frac{12}{13}\right)^2 \\ & = 1 - 2\cdot\frac{144}{169} \\ & = 1 - \frac{288}{169} \\ & = -\frac{119}{169}. \\ & \text{So, the exact value of } \cos\left(2\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{12}{13}\right)\right) \text{ is } -\frac{119}{169}. \end{align*}

Problem 2 (Chapter: 1.5)

Simplify the expression \(\cos(\sin^{-1}(x))\).

Answer:

\( \sqrt{1 - x^2}\)

Solution:

\begin{align*} \text{Step 1:} \quad & \text{We start by recognizing that} \quad \sin^{-1}(x) \text{ is the angle whose sine is} \quad x. \\ \text{Step 2:} \quad & \text{We can then draw a right triangle where the opposite side is} \quad x \quad \text{and the hypotenuse is} \quad 1. \\ & \text{This is because the sine of an angle in a right triangle is the ratio of the length of the opposite side to the length of the hypotenuse.} \\ \text{Step 3:} \quad & \text{Using the Pythagorean theorem, we can find the length of the adjacent side.} \\ & \text{The theorem states that the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides.} \\ & \text{In this case, the hypotenuse is} \quad 1 \quad \text{and one of the other sides is} \quad x. \\ & \text{Therefore, the length of the adjacent side is} \quad \sqrt{1 - x^2}. \\ \text{Step 4:} \quad & \text{The cosine of an angle in a right triangle is the ratio of the length of the adjacent side to the length of the hypotenuse.} \\ & \text{Therefore,} \quad \cos(\sin^{-1}(x)) = \frac{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}{1} = \sqrt{1 - x^2}. \end{align*}

Problem 3 (Chapter: 3.6)

Find the derivative of \(y = \sin(x^2)\).

Answer:

\( 2x \cos(x^2)\)

Solution:

\begin{align*} \text{Step 1:} \quad & \text{Identify the outer function and the inner function.} \\ & \text{In this case, the outer function is} \quad \sin(x) \quad \text{and the inner function is} \quad x^2. \\ \text{Step 2:} \quad & \text{Apply the chain rule.} \\ & \text{The chain rule states that the derivative of a composite function is the derivative of the outer function times the derivative of the inner function.} \\ \text{Step 3:} \quad & \text{Differentiate the outer function.} \\ & \text{The derivative of} \quad \sin(x) \quad \text{is} \quad \cos(x). \\ \text{Step 4:} \quad & \text{Differentiate the inner function.} \\ & \text{The derivative of} \quad x^2 \quad \text{is} \quad 2x. \\ \text{Step 5:} \quad & \text{Multiply the derivatives from steps 3 and 4 according to the chain rule.} \\ & \text{This gives us} \quad (\cos(x^2))(2x). \quad \text{So, the derivative of} \quad y = \sin(x^2) \quad \text{is} \quad y' = 2x \cos(x^2). \end{align*}

Problem 4 (Chapter: 3.6)

If \(f(5) = 6\) and \(f'(5) = 3/4\), find \((f^{-1})'(6)\).

Answer:

\( 4/3 \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{We are given that} \quad f(5) = 6 \quad \text{and} \quad f'(5) = \frac{3}{4}, \quad \text{and we are asked to find the derivative of the inverse function at 6,} \quad (f^{-1})'(6). \\ & \text{We know from the inverse function theorem that the derivative of the inverse function is the reciprocal of the derivative of the function at the corresponding point.} \\ & \text{Mathematically, this is represented as} \quad (f^{-1})'(b) = \frac{1}{f'(a)}, \quad \text{where} \quad f(a) = b. \\ & \text{In this problem, we are given that} \quad f(5) = 6, \quad \text{so we can say that} \quad a = 5 \quad \text{and} \quad b = 6. \\ & \text{Therefore, we can substitute these values into the formula to find} \quad (f^{-1})'(6) = \frac{1}{f'(5)}. \\ & \text{We are given that} \quad f'(5) = \frac{3}{4}, \quad \text{so we substitute this value into the formula to get} \quad (f^{-1})'(6) = \frac{1}{\frac{3}{4}}. \\ & \text{This simplifies to} \quad (f^{-1})'(6) = \frac{4}{3}. \quad \text{So, the derivative of the inverse function at 6,} \quad (f^{-1})'(6), \quad \text{is} \quad \frac{4}{3}. \end{align*}

Problem 5 (Chapter: 3.10)

Find the differential \(dy\) and evaluate \(dy\) for the given values of \(x\) and \(dx\). \(y = e^{3x}, x = 2, dx = 0.3\).

Answer:

\( 0.9e^6 \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{To find the differential} \quad dy, \quad \text{we first need to take the derivative of} \quad y \quad \text{with respect to} \quad x. \\ & \text{The function} \quad y = e^{3x} \quad \text{is a composition of two functions: the exponential function and the function} \quad 3x. \\ & \text{Therefore, we need to use the chain rule to find its derivative.} \\ & \text{The chain rule states that the derivative of a composition of functions is the derivative of the outer function times the derivative of the inner function.} \\ & \text{The outer function is} \quad e^u, \quad \text{and its derivative is} \quad e^u. \\ & \text{The inner function is} \quad 3x, \quad \text{and its derivative is} \quad 3. \\ & \text{So, by the chain rule,} \quad \frac{dy}{dx} = e^{3x} \cdot 3 = 3e^{3x}. \\ & \text{Next, we use the definition of the differential} \quad dy, \quad \text{which is} \quad dy = \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right) \cdot dx. \\ & \text{Substituting the expression we found for} \quad \frac{dy}{dx}, \quad \text{we get} \quad dy = 3e^{3x} \cdot dx. \\ & \text{Finally, we substitute the given values of} \quad x \quad \text{and} \quad dx \quad \text{into the expression for} \quad dy. \\ & \text{We have} \quad x = 2 \quad \text{and} \quad dx = 0.3, \quad \text{so} \quad dy = 3e^{3 \cdot 2} \cdot 0.3 = 3e^6 \cdot 0.3 = 0.9e^6. \end{align*}

Problem 6 (Chapter: 3.10)

Use a linear approximation (or differentials) to estimate the given number \(\sqrt{49.7}\).

Answer:

\( 7.05 \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{Step 1: Identify the function and the point of tangency.} \\ & \text{The function here is} \quad f(x) = \sqrt{x} \quad \text{and the point of tangency we'll use is} \quad a = 49, \quad \text{because it's a perfect square close to} \quad 49.7. \\ & \text{Step 2: Find the derivative of the function.} \\ & \text{The derivative of} \quad f(x) = \sqrt{x} \quad \text{is} \quad f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}. \\ & \text{Step 3: Evaluate the derivative at the point of tangency.} \\ & \text{Substitute} \quad a = 49 \quad \text{into the derivative, we get} \quad f'(49) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{49}} = \frac{1}{14}. \\ & \text{Step 4: Find the equation of the tangent line.} \\ & \text{The equation of the tangent line is} \quad L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a). \\ & \text{Substituting the values we have, we get} \quad L(x) = \sqrt{49} + \frac{1}{14} \cdot (x - 49). \\ & \text{Step 5: Use the linear approximation to estimate} \quad \sqrt{49.7}. \\ & \text{Substitute} \quad x = 49.7 \quad \text{into the equation of the tangent line, we get} \quad L(49.7) = \sqrt{49} + \frac{1}{14} \cdot (49.7 - 49). \\ & \text{After simplifying, we get} \quad L(49.7) = 7 + 0.05 = 7.05. \\ & \text{So, the linear approximation of} \quad \sqrt{49.7} \quad \text{is approximately} \quad 7.05. \end{align*}

Problem 7 (Chapter: 3.10)

Use a linear approximation (or differentials) to estimate the given number \(\cos(44^{\circ})\).

Answer:

\( \sqrt{2}/2(1 - \pi/180) \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{To estimate} \quad \cos\left(\frac{44\pi}{180}\right), \quad \text{we can use a linear approximation by approximating the function near a known point.} \\ & \text{The point we'll use for approximation is} \quad \frac{\pi}{4} \quad \text{because that's a familiar value for cosine and it's near} \quad \frac{44\pi}{180}. \\ & \text{First, we need to find the derivative of cosine, which is} \quad -\sin(x). \\ & \text{The linear approximation formula is:} \quad f(a + \Delta x) \approx f(a) + f'(a) \cdot \Delta x. \\ & \text{The value we're approximating is} \quad \cos\left(\frac{44\pi}{180}\right), \quad \text{which is equivalent to} \quad \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4} + \Delta x\right) \quad \text{with} \quad \Delta x = \frac{44\pi}{180} - \frac{\pi}{4}. \\ & \text{Let's calculate} \quad \Delta x: \quad \Delta x = \frac{44\pi}{180} - \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{11\pi}{45} = \frac{(45 - 44)\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{180}. \\ & \text{Now, we will substitute} \quad a = \frac{\pi}{4}, \quad f(a) = \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right), \quad f'(a) = -\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right), \quad \text{and} \quad \Delta x = \frac{\pi}{180} \\ & \text{into the linear approximation formula:} \\ & f(a + \Delta x) \approx f(a) + f'(a) \cdot \Delta x \approx \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) - \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} \\ & = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{\pi}{180}\right). \end{align*}

Problem 8 (Chapter: 5.3)

Find the derivative of the function \(f(x) = \int_{3x}^{4x} (u^2-3)/(u^2+1)\, du \).

Answer:

\( 4(16x^2-3)/(16x^2+1) - 3(9x^2-3)/(9x^2+1) \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{The problem involves finding the derivative of a function that is defined by an integral.} \\ & \text{We can use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus Part 1,} \\ & \text{which states that if a function} \, f \, \text{is continuous on the interval} \, [a, b] \, \text{and} \, F \, \text{is an antiderivative of} \, f \, \text{on} \, [a, b], \\ & \text{then the definite integral from} \, a \, \text{to} \, b \, \text{of} \, f(x) \, dx \, \text{is equal to} \, F(b) - F(a). \\ & \text{Here, the function inside the integral is} \, f(u) = \frac{u^2 - 3}{u^2 + 1}. \\ & \text{We are asked to find the derivative of the function defined by the integral of} \, f(u) \, \text{from} \, 3x \, \text{to} \, 4x. \\ & \text{By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus Part 1, the derivative of the integral from} \, a(x) \, \text{to} \, b(x) \, \text{of} \, f(t) \, dt \, \text{with respect to} \, x \\ & \text{is} \, f(b(x)) \cdot b'(x) - f(a(x)) \cdot a'(x). \\ & \text{Applying this to our function, we get} \\ & f'(x) = f(4x) \cdot 4 - f(3x) \cdot 3 \\ & = \frac{(4x)^2 - 3}{(4x)^2 + 1} \cdot 4 - \frac{(3x)^2 - 3}{(3x)^2 + 1} \cdot 3 \\ & = \frac{16x^2 - 3}{16x^2 + 1} \cdot 4 - \frac{9x^2 - 3}{9x^2 + 1} \cdot 3. \end{align*}

Problem 10 (Chapter: 5.3)

Find a function \(f\) and a number \(a\) such that \(4 + \int_{a}^{x} f(t)/(t^3)\,dt = \sqrt{x}\) for all \(x > 0\).

Answer:

\( f(x)=x^{5/2}/2, a=16 \)

Solution:

\begin{align*} & \text{To solve this problem, we first identify the given function as the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus Part 1, which states:} \\ & \text{If a function} \, f \, \text{is continuous on the interval} \, [a, b] \, \text{and} \, F \, \text{is an antiderivative of} \, f \, \text{on} \, [a, b], \\ & \text{then} \, \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \\ & \text{So, if we have an equation of the form} \, p + \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \, dt = h(x), \\ & \text{we can say that} \, h'(x) = f(x). \\ & \text{In our case,} \, p = 4, \, h(x) = \sqrt{x}, \, \text{and we are looking for} \, f(x) \, \text{and} \, a. \\ & \text{First, let's find} \, h'(x): \quad h'(x) = \frac{1}{2x^{1/2}}, \quad \text{which simplifies to} \quad h'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}. \\ & \text{Therefore, we have} \, f(x) = h'(x) \cdot (x^3), \quad \text{which simplifies to} \, f(x) = \frac{x^{5/2}}{2}. \\ & \text{Now we need to find} \, a. \, \text{To do this, we use the equation} \, p + \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \, dt = h(x). \\ & \text{Substitute} \, p = 4, \, h(x) = \sqrt{x}, \, \text{and} \, f(x) = \frac{x^{5/2}}{2} \, \text{into the equation:} \\ & 4 + \int_{a}^{x} \frac{x^{5/2}}{2} \, dt = \sqrt{x}. \\ & \text{Since the integral from} \, a \, \text{to} \, a \, \text{is 0, we can write this as:} \\ & 4 + 0 = \sqrt{a}, \quad \text{which simplifies to} \quad a = 16. \\ & \text{So, the function} \, f \, \text{and the number} \, a \, \text{that satisfy the given conditions are} \\ & f(x) = \frac{x^{5/2}}{2} \, \text{and} \, a = 16. \end{align*}
